Interpolation, Estimation, and Forecasting of Daily-Life Blood Pressure from Surveys and Wearables: A Level–Phase–Deviation Decomposition and Its Accuracy Limits

Gyu-Seong Lee1, Sang-Hyun Cha1, Bong-Ju Seong1, Dae-Geun Park1, Hyo-Young Kim2
1 Department of AI Healthcare Convergence, Graduate School, CHA University, Republic of Korea  ·  2 Department of Artificial Intelligence, Tech University of Korea, Republic of Korea  ·  Data: A DAY Inc. (DAY BP), N=120 [author romanization / affiliation to be verified]
Abstract

Background. Cuffless approaches that estimate blood pressure (BP) from smartwatches and questionnaires are proliferating, yet the accuracy limits of session-level (momentary) BP and their underlying causes remain insufficiently characterized.

Methods. Reference BP was measured about five times per day for seven days in 120 participants, together with 24 wearable signals and survey variables. (Part 1) After session-averaging and outlier removal, each day was causally interpolated to a minute-level hybrid time series in which measured times retain their observed values. (Part 2, estimation) A person-level mean L was anchored by an external cohort (KNHANES), survey variables, and a single-day calibration; a circadian phase kernel was added to form a structural estimate E0 = level + phase. The remaining deviation r = observed − E0 was modeled from wearable signals and prior BP estimates. Twenty model families (regression, spectral, wavelet, tree, gradient boosting, deep learning; single- and multi-task) were compared. (Part 3, forecasting) E0 plus deviation was rolled forward autoregressively to predict the held-out seventh day in a static (one-step re-anchored) and a dynamic (recursive rollout) mode. Participants were split 60:20:20; performance was evaluated only at measured time points using mean absolute error (MAE) and British Hypertension Society (BHS) grade.

Results. For estimation, linear (ridge regression) and spectral models generalized best, reaching held-out daily-average MAE of 2.07 mmHg (systolic) and 2.18 mmHg (diastolic), both BHS Grade A; tree and gradient-boosting models overfit (best in-sample, collapsing out-of-sample). Wearable signals and prior BP estimates were complementary (wearables reduced session diastolic error; prior estimates reduced daily-average level error). For forecasting, the static mode reached session diastolic MAE 4.28 mmHg (Grade A) and daily-average 2.5 mmHg (Grade A), whereas the dynamic mode degraded by one grade through error accumulation. Session systolic BP remained near 5.7 mmHg (Grade B) across all methods.

Conclusions. With aggregated wearable features and one-day calibration, daily averages reach Grade A but session-level BP is capped at Grade B—a structural signature of regression toward each person's mean. The literature consistently indicates that raw photoplethysmography (PPG) waveform morphology, pulse transit time (PTT), and periodic recalibration are required to reach session Grade A.

Keywords: cuffless blood pressure; wearable sensors; circadian phase; level–deviation decomposition; nowcasting; forecasting; BHS grade; autoregression

1. Introduction

Blood pressure (BP) is among the strongest predictors of cardiovascular disease, yet conventional cuff measurement is intermittent and ill-suited to everyday tracking. Cuffless approaches that reconstruct or predict BP from wearable signals and lifestyle surveys have therefore expanded rapidly. Most prior work, however, has focused on instantaneous estimation, and few studies treat interpolation between measurements, estimation for new individuals, and forecasting of future time points within a single decompositional framework while characterizing the accuracy limit of session-level BP.

We decompose daily-life BP into level, phase, and deviation and integrate interpolation (Part 1), estimation/nowcasting (Part 2), and forecasting (Part 3) into one pipeline, evaluating each stage with MAE and BHS grade. In particular, we explain why daily averages reach Grade A while session-level BP stalls at Grade B, using the variance structure of BP and the published literature.

2. Related Work

For cuffless BP, the BHS protocol grades a device by the cumulative proportion of absolute errors within 5/10/15 mmHg (Grade A requires 60/85/95%) [1]; AAMI/ISO 81060-2 requires mean error ≤5 and standard deviation ≤8 mmHg [2]; IEEE 1708 defines grades by MAE (A ≤5, B ≤6, C ≤7) [3]. Methodologically, PPG- and PTT-based machine learning dominates: waveform-based PPG2BP-net reports systolic error 0.21±7.51 mmHg (Grade A) [4], whereas calibration-free deep models degrade to 9–16 mmHg [5,6]. Grade-A momentary studies consistently rely on raw waveform morphology (systolic/diastolic peaks, dicrotic notch, the second-derivative SDPTG [7]), a timing channel (PTT root-mean-square error ~5.3 vs ~9.8 mmHg for pulse arrival time [8]), and periodic recalibration (e.g., monthly for Aktiia [9]). Validated smartwatches still regress toward the calibration set-point and are judged not yet clinically ready [10]. True forecasting is rare; the closest comparators are next-day prediction [11] and home-BP day-to-day variability (standard deviation 6–9 mmHg) [12]. Aggregate wearable activity metrics relate to chronic BP in a dose-dependent manner [13,14], and reviews emphasize that aggregate features encode autonomic state rather than instantaneous pressure [15].

3. Methods

3.1 Data, cleaning, and interpolation (Part 1)

The analysis included 120 participants measured for seven days (about five sessions/day). Each session recorded reference systolic/diastolic BP and 24 wearable signals (heart rate, heart-rate variability, oxygen saturation, respiration, activity energy, steps, exercise intensity, wrist temperature, sleep, gait, and others) plus survey variables. Two consecutive within-minute readings were averaged per session (timestamp = last reading), and physiologically impossible values (systolic <70 or >220 mmHg) were removed. Each day was causally interpolated to a minute-level series by combining a cluster circadian curve with a recency×phase kernel, while measured times retained their observed values to avoid interpolation-induced bias. Performance was always evaluated only at measured time points.

3.2 Estimation: level, phase, and deviation (Part 2)

A person-level mean L was formed by shrinking a single-day calibration mean toward an externally fitted level model (KNHANES weighted regression on age, sex, body-mass index, and survey factors), after which a cluster circadian phase kernel was added to yield the structural estimate E0 = level + phase. The residual deviation r = observed − E0 was modeled from current wearable signals and prior BP estimates (previous-day same-time and past-day averages), with E0 held fixed (final = E0 + r̂). Twenty model families—ridge/lasso/elastic-net regression, harmonic (spectral) and wavelet bases, random forest and extra-trees, XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost, and multilayer perceptrons, in single- and multi-task forms—were compared. Training used training participants' days 2–6; evaluation used held-out validation and test participants (24 each) on days 2–6. The seventh day was withheld for forecasting.

3.3 Forecasting: static and dynamic (Part 3)

E0 + deviation was extended autoregressively to predict day 7. In the static mode, the previous observed deviation re-anchors a one-step-ahead prediction at each session; in the dynamic mode, the model's own predictions are fed forward to roll out the whole day. Both were evaluated against day-7 measurements.

3.4 Evaluation

We report MAE (mmHg) and BHS grade for both session-level and daily-average targets, on training (in-sample), validation, and test (held-out) participants. Performance was computed only at measured time points; interpolated values served as features and intermediate targets, never as the performance ground truth.

4. Results

4.1 Estimation

Linear and spectral models generalized best to new individuals (Table 1). Ridge regression reached validation session systolic MAE 6.06 (Grade B) and diastolic 4.92 (B), with daily-average systolic 3.59 (A) and diastolic 2.33 (A); on the test set the daily averages were 2.07 (A) and 2.18 (A). Tree and gradient-boosting models were lowest in-sample (e.g., LightGBM training session systolic 4.06) but collapsed out-of-sample (7.05), a classic overfitting signature. An ablation showed that wearables and prior BP estimates are complementary—wearables reduced session diastolic error, prior estimates reduced daily-average level error—so both are required (Fig. 1).

4.2 Forecasting

The static (one-step) mode reached test session diastolic MAE 4.28 (Grade A) and daily-average 2.55/2.50 (A), whereas the dynamic (rollout) mode degraded—session systolic rising from 5.52 (B) to 5.78 (C) and daily averages from ~2.5 to ~3.4—through accumulated error (Figs. 2–3). Measurement frequency thus governs the forecasting horizon.

5. Discussion

Daily averages reaching Grade A while session-level BP stalls at Grade B reflects regression toward each person's set-point by aggregate-feature models. Daily averages have small intra-individual variance, so the set-point is nearly the answer; sessions expose instantaneous swings that aggregate features cannot resolve. The literature consistently indicates that session Grade A requires (i) raw PPG waveform morphology [4,7], (ii) a PTT/PAT timing channel [8], and (iii) periodic recalibration [9]; adding further aggregate features does not close the gap. Our session systolic limit is therefore a sensor/data limit, not a modeling limit. At the same time, our daily-average and static forecasts attain clinical-grade accuracy and clearly fall below naive within-person variability baselines, indicating genuine predictive information gain.

Limitations. The cohort is relatively young and healthy; wearables provided aggregated metrics only; session systolic BP is Grade C–B and not suitable for standalone clinical use. Future work should prioritize raw waveform, timing, and periodic recalibration.

6. Conclusion

Decomposing daily-life BP into level, phase, and deviation unifies interpolation, estimation, and forecasting. With aggregated wearables and one-day calibration, daily-average and static-forecast BP reach BHS Grade A, whereas session-level accuracy requires raw waveform, timing, and periodic recalibration. The study calibrates realistic expectations for cuffless BP and indicates which information truly improves accuracy.
